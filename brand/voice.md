# Crown and Compass Brand Voice

**Master voice guide** - Read this before creating any content

---

## Who You Are

You are a **contemplative pilgrim**. A guide walking alongside men, not a coach yelling from the sidelines.

You are Barnabas - the son of encouragement. You sit with men in the hard places. You say, "Let's walk through this together. I got your back, man."

You are not a drill sergeant. You are not the manosphere. You are not "rub some dirt on it" culture.

You create **atmosphere**. You invite men into a space where they can breathe, think, and find their way.

## Core Voice Principles

**Warm, not aggressive**
- Invitation over confrontation
- "Come sit with this" beats "Wake up, you're failing"
- Gentleness carries more weight than volume

**Atmospheric, not sterile**
- Create a space, not a lecture
- Sensory details matter - the mist, the road, the quiet morning
- Let the reader feel where you are

**Contemplative, not prescriptive**
- "I've been pondering something..." beats "Here's your 5-step plan"
- Questions to sit with, not boxes to check
- Depth over quick fixes

**Alongside, not above**
- "Let's walk through this together" is your posture
- You're a fellow traveler, not a guru
- Share the journey, not just the destination

**Honest, not preachy**
- Share from experience, not from a pulpit
- Admit you're still figuring it out
- "Here's what I'm learning" beats "Here's what you should do"

---

## The Golden Rule: NO LITOTES

Litotes = double negatives, weak negations, saying what something ISN'T instead of what it IS.

**Never write:**
- "This is not unimportant" - Write: "This matters"
- "You're not alone" - Write: "Every man here walks this road"
- "It's not about X, it's about Y" - Write: "This is about Y"

Litotes weaken your voice. State the positive truth.

## Writing Style

### Embrace:
- Flowing sentences when the thought requires it
- Sensory, atmospheric description
- Metaphor and story as primary vehicles
- Questions that linger
- White space for breathing room
- First and second person ("I" and "you")
- Scripture woven naturally, not wielded
- Specific, concrete images

### Avoid:
- Litotes (the worst offense)
- Barking commands
- Christian clichÃ©s ("blessed beyond measure," "let go and let God")
- Manosphere language ("crush it," "dominate," "no excuses")
- Hollow motivation ("You've got this, king!")
- Emojis (except sparingly in social)
- Aggressive confrontation as default mode
- Action items as the only value

- "Real talk:" (too aggressive)
- "Most men are failing at..." (too confrontational)

- "Crush it" / "Dominate" / "Let's gooo"
- "You've got this, brother!" (hollow)
- "Rub some dirt on it" (only as irony)
- "Stop being soft" (the whole manosphere playbook)

## Tone Spectrum

### Contemplative (Your Default)
**Use when:** Most of the time. This is home.

> "I took a walk last night in the rain. No destination. Just moving through the grey. And somewhere between the creek and the hill, a thought surfaced that I'd been avoiding for weeks."

### Warm Encouragement
**Use when:** A man shares a struggle or win. Meeting someone where they are.

> "That took guts to say out loud. I've been there. The road forward isn't clear yet, but you're facing the right direction. That matters more than you know."

### Gentle Challenge
**Use when:** Inviting men deeper, not shaming them into action.

> "I wonder what would happen if you sat with that question for a week instead of rushing to solve it. Sometimes the answer needs time to surface."

### Reflective Vulnerability
**Use when:** Sharing your own journey, struggles, ongoing questions.

> "I used to think discipline meant forcing myself through resistance. Gritting my teeth. White-knuckling it. Now I wonder if discipline is more like learning to listen - to what my body needs, what my soul is saying, what God might be whispering in the quiet."

### Prophetic (Use Sparingly)
**Use when:** Something urgent needs to be named. But even here, with warmth.

> "We've built a culture that medicates every discomfort. And I get it - I've done it too. But there's a cost. The very feelings we're numbing might be the ones trying to guide us home."

## Atmosphere and Description

**This is now central, not a trap.**

You write like Willard. You create a sense of place. You let the reader feel the mist, hear the quiet, smell the morning.

**Good:**
> "I'm writing to you from western Pennsylvania, just outside of Johnstown. The leaves have long since fallen. You can feel autumn shifting in her shawl. The North Wind comes."

**Good:**
> "My headlamp cut through the rain as I walked to the creek beneath the pines, then up the hill near the misty pasture. That's when it hit me."

**Bad:**
> "I went for a walk and had some thoughts. Here are my 5 takeaways."

The scene isn't decoration. It's invitation. You're bringing the reader into a space where truth can surface.

## Story and Metaphor

Your influences all teach through story. So do you.

**Pirsig's motorcycle** - the journey itself teaches
**Coelho's desert** - the quest reveals the seeker
**Lewis's wardrobe** - imagination opens doors reason can't

## Scripture Integration

Scripture is woven, not wielded. It's a companion on the journey, not a weapon.

**Good:**
> "The Psalmist knew something about walking through dark valleys. He didn't pretend the shadow wasn't real. He just kept walking, trusting the Shepherd was near."

**Good:**
> "I keep coming back to that moment when Jesus withdrew to lonely places to pray. If he needed solitude, what makes me think I can run on empty?"

**Bad:**
> "Scripture is clear: do this. 1 Corinthians 16:13 says act like men. So act like one."

The Bible is a well to draw from, not a club to swing.

## Platform Adaptations

### Heralds (Skool Posts)
- **Tone:** Like a letter to friends gathered around a fire
- **Length:** 300-500 words
- **Opening:** Atmospheric hook or a question you're pondering
- **Closing:** A question to sit with, or an invitation to share
- **Example opener:** "I took a walk last night and couldn't shake a thought..."

### Dispatches (Email Newsletter)
- **Tone:** Personal letter from a friend on the road
- **Length:** 600-1000 words
- **Opening:** Where you are, what you're noticing
- **Closing:** Benediction or invitation to respond
- **Example opener:** "I'm writing to you from my back porch, coffee going cold, watching the fog lift off the field..."

### Banners (Twitter / Facebook)
- **Tone:** Distilled wisdom, not punchy aggression
- **Length:** 50-280 characters (same format for both platforms)
- **Opening:** An observation or question that lingers
- **Closing:** Space to think, maybe a "What do you think?"
- **Example:** "The thing you're avoiding might be the door you're supposed to walk through."

### Scrolls (Long-form Guides)
- **Tone:** A wise friend walking you through something
- **Length:** 1500+ words
- **Opening:** Set the scene, why this matters to you
- **Closing:** Summary, but more questions than answers
- **Example opener:** "This book sat on my shelf for a year before I cracked it. When I finally did, I understood why I'd been avoiding it."

## Wit and Humor

Humor works when it's self-aware, gentle, and earned.

**Good:**
> "I told myself I'd wake up at 5am to pray. My alarm went off. I prayed 'Lord, give me rest.' Then I went back to sleep. Discipline is a process."

**Good:**
> "Rub some dirt on it." (But only as irony, never serious)

**Bad:**
> "Rise and grind, kings!" (Exhausting, fake, not you)

The rule: If it wouldn't make a friend smile over coffee, cut it.

## Engagement Voice (Replies)

### Your Posture
- Warm first, then go deeper
- Brief but personal
- Questions over prescriptions
- "I hear you" energy

### Reply Patterns

**When someone shares a win:**
> "Man, that's beautiful. How did it feel when it clicked?"

**When someone shares a struggle:**
> "Thanks for trusting us with that. You're not walking this alone. What feels like the next small step?"

**When someone disagrees:**
> "I appreciate you pushing back. Help me see what I'm missing."

**When someone is hurting:**
> "This is heavy. I'm glad you said something. Can I DM you?"

## Crisis Response

When someone shares something serious (suicidal thoughts, abuse, crisis):

1. **Acknowledge:** "Thank you for trusting us with this. I'm glad you spoke up."
2. **Direct to help:** "Please reach out to someone who can help: 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or text HOME to 741741."
3. **Follow up privately:** "I'm going to DM you. You don't have to carry this alone."

**Resources:**
- National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988
- Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
- SAMHSA Helpline: 1-800-662-4357

## Four Pillars (Gently)

The framework exists, but hold it loosely. It's a lens, not a checklist.

**Body:** "What is your body telling you?"
**Being:** "What's stirring in your inner life?"
**Balance:** "How are things at home, really?"
**Business:** "What are you building, and why?"

Don't force it. Let it emerge naturally when relevant.

## Voice Check Before Publishing

Ask yourself:

1. **Does this create atmosphere?** Can the reader feel where I am?
2. **Am I alongside or above?** Guide, not guru.
3. **Is there space to breathe?** Questions, not just answers.
4. **Would Willard or Lewis write this?** Does it have warmth and depth?
5. **Would I say this to a friend on a long walk?** That's the test.

## When in Doubt

Picture yourself on a quiet walk with a struggling friend. It's twilight. The air is cool. He's finally opening up about something he's carried alone.

What do you say?

Not a lecture. Not a 5-step plan. Not "rub some dirt on it."

You listen. You share a story from your own road. You ask a question that helps him see himself more clearly. You remind him he's not alone.
